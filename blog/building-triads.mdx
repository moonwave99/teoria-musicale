One should first interiorise how to play any triad. Since triads are made of thirds, let's build them.

<Truncate/>

## Types of thirds

All pair of notes that are three note names apart is a third (e.g. `A-C`, `E-Gb`).
The ones needed for building (most) chords in triadic harmony are simply:

- **Major**: 3M (e.g. `C-E`);
- **Minor**: 3m (e.g. `F-Ab`).

The combinations on the keyboard may look a very huge number, but we can reduce them down to a very small amount.
Since the piano is a mono-dimensional instrument with keys of just two colors, the permutations will just be:

- white to white (WW);
- white to black (WB);
- black to white (BW);
- black to black (BB).

## Stacking thirds

We know how to build triads:

<Table
  headers={['Type', 'Intervals', 'Examples']}
  rows={[
    ['Major', '3M + 3m', 'C-E-G, Eb-G-Bb'],
    ['Minor', '3m + 3M', 'C-Eb-G, Eb-Gb-Bb'],
    ['Dimished', '3m + 3m', 'C-Eb-Gb, Eb-Gb-Bbb'],
    ['Augmented', '3M + 3M', 'C-E-G#, Eb-G-B'],
]}/>

If we were to memorise all root-type combinations we would end up with 12 * 4 = 48 (!) triads: we will see how we can reduce the complexity a lot.

Let's arrange our work in subgroups:

- major / root on white keys;
- major / root on black keys;
- minor / root on white keys;
- minor / root on black keys;
- diminshed;
- augmented.

:::note What have we learned

We boiled down 48 entries to 6 + 6 + 3 + 4 = 19, not bad! Let's not forget that some shapes are relatively less used (augmented chords, I am looking at you!),
and it's not a crime to let them a little in the background of our memory. The other three types are crucial for building **seventh cords**, so make sure to ingrain them in your fingers.

:::
